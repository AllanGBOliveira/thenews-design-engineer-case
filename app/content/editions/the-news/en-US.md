## The fashion that became identity

A few years ago, wearing a brand's t-shirt was simply a sign of purchasing power. Today, the logic has completely changed. The clothes you wear communicate who you **identify with**, not how much you **spent**.

A Michelin-starred restaurant in California launched a hoodie that sold out in minutes and became a collector's item. [Bell's Kitchen](#), the establishment in question, has never spent a cent on traditional advertising — but has a 3-month waitlist.

> **In the end, the t-shirt with the giant logo of a traditional luxury brand lost ground to the hoodie from that hyped cafeteria** or your favorite content creator. The top of the wardrobe now belongs to whoever can generate real connection.

## The knockout stage begins

::article-image{src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1579952363873-27f3bade9f55?w=720&q=80" alt="Brazilian players celebrating a World Cup goal" caption="(Image: REUTERS | Dylan Martinez)"}

**Today officially marks the start of the round of 32 at the World Cup.** In case you're lost — with so many more teams in this edition, the group stage changed and an extra knockout round was needed before the round of 16.

- **Who advances:** The top 2 finishers from each of the 12 groups (24 teams) + the 8 best third-place finishers overall.
- Combining the 24 direct qualifiers with the 8 best third-place teams gives exactly **32 nations** — facing each other in single-elimination games (16 matches).

The match opening this round is Canada vs. South Africa at 4 PM. ***Tomorrow, it's Brazil's turn — they face Japan at 2 PM.***

## The most talked-about pause at the World Cup

::article-image{src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1522778119026-d647f0596c20?w=720&q=80" alt="Player pouring water on himself during hydration break" caption="(Image: Lynne Sladky | AP)"}

Something is happening at the Cup that's annoying some fans — and it has nothing to do with red cards or players wasting time. **Yes, it's the two hydration breaks that take place during matches.**

> **But it was in that short 3-minute window** that the advertising market spotted the perfect opportunity to shine.

- Fox paid between **$400 million and $500 million** for broadcast rights to the entire tournament — and commercials shown during hydration breaks alone will bring the network between **$250 million and $600 million**.
- Basically, brands like Nike, Adidas, and Coca-Cola will cover Fox's entire tournament bill just in those few minutes of break.
